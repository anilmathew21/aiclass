Here are three curated sets of research papers. Each set provides a perfect "case file" for your Argus Engine to investigate, as they contain related concepts, evolving ideas, and different perspectives.Case File 1: The Dawn of the TransformerThis collection traces the invention and rise of the Transformer architecture, the foundational technology behind all modern LLMs. Your Argus Engine could create a timeline, compare the key innovations of each paper, or explain how the ideas evolved.Attention Is All You Need (Vaswani et al., 2017)The Spark: This is the legendary paper that introduced the Transformer model, arguing that self-attention mechanisms alone could outperform the dominant RNN/LSTM models.BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding (Devlin et al., 2018)The Evolution: This paper showed how to effectively pre-train a deep Transformer model on a massive amount of text, leading to huge leaps in performance on a wide range of language tasks.Language Models are Few-Shot Learners (Brown et al., 2020)The Scale: This is the famous GPT-3 paper. It demonstrated that by massively scaling up a Transformer model, it could perform new tasks from just a few examples ("few-shot learning") without any additional training.Case File 2: The AI Alignment DebateThis set of papers explores the critical and complex field of AI alignment—the challenge of ensuring that powerful AI systems behave in line with human values. This is a great test for the Argus Engine to compare different proposed solutions and philosophical standpoints.AI Alignment: A Comprehensive Survey (Ji et al., 2024)The Overview: An extremely detailed survey of the entire field. This document provides the foundational concepts and terminology for the investigation.Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback (Bai et al., 2022)A Proposed Solution: Anthropic's paper on training a helpful and harmless AI by having it learn from a "constitution" of principles, rather than constant human feedback.Debate: A Proposal for AI Safety (Irving et al., 2018)A Different Approach: A paper from researchers at OpenAI and DeepMind proposing a method where two AIs debate each other to help a human judge find the truth, even on topics the human doesn't understand.Case File 3: The Mystery of Emergent AbilitiesThis collection dives into the fascinating and debated phenomenon of "emergent abilities" in LLMs—complex skills that seem to appear suddenly in large models but not in smaller ones. Your Argus Engine can synthesize the arguments for and against this idea.Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models (Wei et al., 2022)The Original Thesis: The influential paper that first defined and documented the phenomenon of emergence, arguing that quantitative increases in scale lead to unpredictable qualitative leaps in ability.Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage? (Schaeffer et al., 2023)The Counter-Argument: This paper from Stanford researchers challenges the original thesis, arguing that these so-called "emergent" abilities are often just an illusion created by the choice of metrics, and that performance actually scales smoothly and predictably.Emergent Abilities in Large Language Models: A Survey (Berti et al., 2025)The Synthesis: A more recent survey that reviews the entire debate, analyzing the different definitions of emergence and the evidence from both sides of the argument.
